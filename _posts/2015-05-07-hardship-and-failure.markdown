title:  "Hardship and Failure"

There are innumerable quotes out there about conquering hardship and failure. Nietzsche and various pop stars will tell you “what
doesn’t kill you makes you stronger”. You read stories of entrepreneurs who were on the verge of bankruptcy, of inventors who
failed time and time again, only to prove the doubters wrong and become wildly successful in the end. Like many others, I find
these quotes to be very uplifting given that life is in fact not always easy or fair. Bad things happen, and we all find examples
of our fellow humans prevailing in the face of adversity to be comforting, because we want to believe that we too could rise to
the occasion if needed. However, we must be aware that these aphorisms (which I will refer to as the Narrative of Humanity) play
off our emotions and may cause us to behave irrationally, whereas taking statistical realities into account can result in more
prudent decisions.

We see hardship as a catalyst for escaping complacency or unfavorable situations. But just as intimidation is not the ideal form
of persuasion, hardship is not the ideal motivator of change. While hardship can change your perspective and realign your goals,
it does so at the expense of your physical, emotional, or financial health. And sometimes, it comes with no benefits and only
costs. Suppose you grew up in a broken home, with one or both parents being alcoholics. You worked two jobs while in high school
to support yourself and your little brother, all while graduating at the top of your class and going on to becoming a successful
doctor, lawyer, CEO, or what have you. Your difficult upbringing gave you trust issues and a chip on your shoulder, but it also
taught you about hard work and service to others. If this sounds like you, then you are most likely not real and are instead a
television character, and I have a future as a writer. Here’s a more likely version: you sacrificed your time with friends and
becoming socially adjusted; your money providing for your brother because your parents cannot; your physical health from the long
hours of work and study; and your emotional well-being from seeing your parents fight, thus forcing you to go through all of this.
And to top it off, you are…completely average. You’re no better than your peers, yet you still have the aforementioned baggage,
thereby making your life strictly worse than anyone else’s. And why wouldn’t it be? It may be possible that your situation lights
a fire inside of you and propels you to overcompensate (like the TV character), but I would say it’s far more plausible that you
would perform worse compared to an unencumbered version of yourself. But not only is this scenario less interesting, it’s also
just plain depressing. Instead, we prefer the TV character version that indulges our romanticization of the underdog. If suffering
is a part of life, we do not want to see examples of people who had nothing to show for it. It just wouldn’t play nicely into the
Narrative of Humanity that we’ve constructed.

There is an economic corollary to the thought that bad things beget the good. Unfortunately it’s a fallacy, namely the broken
window fallacy. Taking the broken window fallacy to its logical extreme would mean that we could arrive at economic prosperity by
destroying all of our assets. And taking “what doesn’t kill you makes you stronger” to its logical extreme would mean that
physical and emotional torture would make you Superman. You may object to the dealing in absolutes, which I can understand.
However, I find even small steps in those directions to be undesirable. Wealth and happiness are both things that we seek in life.
I would not want to put a dent in my car as a motivator for me to make more money any more than I would wish for a more difficult
childhood as a motivator for me to be a kinder person.

Failure can be thought of as the subset of hardship that we inflict upon ourselves for attempting to realize our goals. Persisting
towards goals is virtuous since humanity would be in a far lesser place otherwise. What disturbs me though is that some people
have almost built a cult of failure, as if failure is itself something to be revered. In the startup world, you hear about
“failing fast”. People will tell you to love failure, and that if you haven’t ever failed then you haven’t learned anything. But
that’s just it – failure is a means of learning, which is a tool for success but is not success itself. Consider a hypothetical
scenario where two parallel universe twins each founded companies that ended up being equally successful, but the second twin did
so with fewer failures along the way (perhaps due to small stroke of luck). I would certainly prefer to be the second twin here.
You may rationalize this by saying that the first twin’s additional failures “taught him more” or “made him a better person”, but
these are subjective benefits. I could certainly counter with subjective costs like emotional stress, as any founder who has woken
up every morning wondering how to keep the company afloat can attest to. And there are certainly objective costs, which include
time and money thrown down a rat hole. So if you were to control for the outcome, then it should be clear that failure is a bad
thing. Of course, we often can’t control for or predict outcomes, and taking this conclusion literally and opting for a strategy
that blindly avoids failure is shortsighted to say the least. But in the case of founding a startup where failure is far more
likely and the financial and emotional toll can range from painful to downright ruinous, I would have a hard time telling people
to love failure with a straight face.

It’s important to remember that success, not failure, is the ultimate goal. We merely tolerate failure as a price to pay for
success. If we took away all success and had only failure in life, then we would be lost. We do not admire people who have only
ever known failure – we hate, mock, and pity them. We admire people who achieve success eventually, whether it means they got it
right on the first try or they failed a hundred times before doing so. We look to the scrappy and determined person who stays in
the game long enough to win big because that expands the pool of people who can be successful. Otherwise, success would be an
exclusive club only for the lucky, privileged, or gifted, which is another notion that would not sit well with us and would
conflict with the Narrative of Humanity.

I offer these thoughts as counterpoints and an alternative perspective to mainstream beliefs that I find to be tired and cliched.
It’s not to say that these mainstream sayings are entirely without merit, but I do think that we look to them more often than we
should, as if we were intellectually papering over things. The truth is that setbacks and hardships do have an impact on people’s
well-being, and the Narrative of Humanity simply does not apply to most people. It bothers me when I see someone who has suffered
in life and sheepishly says that his troubled past made him a better person when he clearly would be better off if he had grown up
without such hardships. The reality of suffering is that most people who have had disadvantaged upbringings continue to lead
disadvantaged lives. There are certainly prominent counterexamples, but that doesn’t mean I’ll be placing my bets on someone who
grew up in the hood any time soon. It also eats away at a nerve every time someone tells me that “at least I learned something”
from my startup failure – pithy remarks like that suggest that the person does not understand the toll that failure takes on a
person. The reality of failure is that most people who attempt great things will fail. However, there’s nothing wrong with that. I
would say that being a good person, friend, or employee is a great success in its own right. For most of us, it is a wiser choice
to enjoy these everyday foundational successes, rather than deluding ourselves into thinking that we are destined for incredible
outcomes and setting ourselves up for bitterness and disappointment.

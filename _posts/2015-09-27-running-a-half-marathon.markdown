A couple of months ago, I ran in the first half of the San Francisco marathon. It was my first serious organized race. My goal was
to finish in under two hours, and my official finish time was 1:57:23. Yay. Like others who ran in the race, I felt a sense of
accomplishment having done it, and the fact that I had hit my goal made it that much more satisfying. And while I surely had some
feelings that were similar to what others experienced, I also wanted to reflect on some thoughts that I doubt other runners had,
if only because of my own peculiarities.

Running in an organized race was never particularly high on my bucket list, but the notion of pushing my body to new limits had
popped in and out of my head a few times before. I never gave it serious consideration though, since I was in decent enough shape
and figured that there’s no need to torture yourself just to prove something. In fact, I thought all of the people who subjected
themselves to simultaneous, coordinated suffering to be idiots (and still do for the most part). So why did I join the idiot crew
myself? One reason, simply put, is that I’m getting older. Regardless of how meaningful I thought it was to reach one’s physical
limits, I couldn’t escape the fact that time only moves in one direction – it would gradually wear on my body and make it more and
more difficult to even ask the question of whether or not I could. I was like an aging couple that started to second-guess their
decision to not have children.

As another consequence of getting older, it also didn’t help that I had gained a fair amount of weight over the past year. Not
enough to be really noticeable to others, but enough to where I had to buy pants a couple of sizes larger, and to where I would
compare pictures of myself from years past and feel a mild sense of disappointment. Some weight gain can be attributed to a
slowdown in the metabolism as one ages, but the fact of the matter was that my lifestyle had also become sloppier. I had a general
feeling that my eating habits had declined, but I never really tracked my food intake, so it wasn’t all that obvious what I had
changed over the years (though one thing to note is that I now consume far more beer than I used to even a year or two ago).
Another obvious change was that after I moved to the city, I all but stopped playing volleyball. And so I figured that running a
half-marathon would kill two birds with stone: checking off a bucket list item, and restoring cardiovascular exercise into my
routine to lose some weight. Now, it’s a fairly well-known fact that doing steady-state, low-intensity cardio is not an efficient
method for losing weight, and it’s also a form of exercise I happen to find to be incredibly dull and painful. But I made that
pill easier to swallow by reminding myself that it’s good to keep an open mind and try new forms of exercise. And besides, I
wasn’t doing any cardio to begin with, so anything was better than nothing! It’s like I always say, the best exercise routine is
the one that actually gets done.

Another motivating factor behind deciding to run in the race was one that can be said of all exercise in general – you get what
you put in. This truism resonated with me because to me it meant that exercise is something that is 100% in your control. I’ve had
ups and downs in my life, especially in my career. There were times when I felt that that opportunities were scarce or
unavailable, and that the people around me were unsupportive or just plain awful. Of course, plenty of that blame lies squarely on
my shoulders. But I can confidently say that I’ve been in some really shitty environments where the deck was stacked against me. A
defining characteristic of a suboptimal environment is one where one’s maximal efforts result in little to no change, be it in
oneself or one’s surroundings. This simply does not hold when it comes to exercise. Everyone may have different physical limits,
but one’s progress is defined entirely by the self. Sabotage and politics are non-factors, axiomatically. This was a refreshing
change of pace from the rest of life. It was exhilarating to know that if I were to fail to meet my goal, or just wuss out from
doing the race entirely, I would have no one to blame or to answer to but myself. Likewise, if I were to succeed in my goal, I
could attribute it entirely to my own efforts, not to circumstance, coalition building, or dumb luck. Most things in life are not
like this (nor would I necessarily want them to be), but it’s nice to have at least a few that are guaranteed wins.

The last and possibly most unusual reason for why I did this was because everyone else was – or rather, it was in *spite* of that.
You might think that spite would be an insane reason to haul your ass out of bed five days a week (three of those started the day
at 7:30am), with one of those days containing a double-digit mile run, and you would be absolutely right. I chalk it up to one of
my personality traits, which is the desire to be unique or otherwise stand out in some way. Thus, to participate in an organized
race, an activity that pervades the health-conscious yuppie culture that surrounds me here in San Francisco, seemed antithetical
to my values. I had a hard time stomaching the idea that it wouldn’t matter how personal or noble my own goals were, since I would
only see myself as one of *those* people. You know exactly who I’m talking about. The people who post their latest Strava or
RunKeeper routes on Facebook to show that they’re living such fit lives. The people in “running clubs” who wear ridiculous outfits
that are somehow both coordinated and mismatching at the same time, as if to show that running were such a dreadful ordeal that it
requires clownery and high-fives with “you go dude/girl!” in order to endure it. The people who sign up for a 10k and finish at a
leisurely time of 1.5 hours, since it would be a shame to not take a break every mile or so to take a selfie and enjoy the view.
The people who participate in the Reebok® Spartan Race™, which consists of a bunch of pudgy buffoons pitifully tossing plastic
javelins at cardboard targets. In other words, the people for whom physical exercise has become a commercialized and banal
lifestyle that includes an utter lack of self-awareness. These are the people and the culture that I decided to reject, in my own
quiet, ironic, and self-harming way.

Nevertheless, running the race left me with some delightful memories and lessons: the anticipation of walking to the Ferry
Building and shuffling into the starting corral while it was still dark outside; the rhythmic splendor of thousands of feet
pitter-pattering along the Embarcadero, with not a single word uttered; the cool serenity of running across the Golden Gate bridge
in the mist as the sun just started to light the sky; the sudden hitting of a wall, with the last mile seeming harder than the
previous twelve combined; the annoyance I felt towards the announcer when he butchered my name as I crossed the finish line; the
satisfaction and relief from it all being over, the culmination of two months of sweat and aches; the peace I felt in my soul as I
showered and then collapsed into bed, seeing that my efforts were rewarded, as if a small, personal justice were served; and
finally, the humility I felt when I realized I didn’t have it all figured out after all, that the unity and support of others is
good, and that there may actually be something to the idea of participating in something that is bigger than yourself. It would
just be nice if it were less tiring.
